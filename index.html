📝 Step 1: Patient Registration

Registration is the collection of patient demographic information, insurance details, consent forms, and identification. This data populates the claim form and the patient account. Every field matters—a single wrong digit in a subscriber ID causes a denial.

Collect and verify: full legal name, DOB, address, phone, insurance card (front and back), subscriber ID, group number, employer, photo ID, signed consent forms, and Assignment of Benefits (AOB).
Front-desk staff are the gatekeepers of data quality. The billing team downstream depends entirely on the accuracy of what is entered at registration. This is a shared responsibility between clinical and administrative staff.

✅ Correct! The insurance card provides the payer name, plan type, subscriber/member ID, group number, copay amounts, and payer contact information. All of this flows directly into the CMS-1500 claim form. Without it, the claim cannot be submitted accurately.
❌ The answer is B. The insurance card provides critical billing data—payer name, subscriber ID, group number, and plan details. This information goes directly onto the claim form. Medical history and diagnosis are clinical matters; fees are determined by contracts and code sets.

🔍 Step 2: Eligibility Verification

Eligibility verification confirms the patient's insurance is active, identifies covered benefits, determines copay/deductible/coinsurance amounts, checks for preauthorization requirements, and establishes coordination of benefits (COB) if multiple insurances exist.

Verify: active coverage, effective/termination dates, plan type, copay amount, deductible status, coinsurance percentage, out-of-pocket maximum, preauthorization requirements, referral needs, COB/primary vs. secondary.
Eligibility verification is the single most impactful front-end step for preventing denials. Industry data shows that eligibility-related denials are among the most common and most preventable claim rejections.
Recognition

Which of the following is verified during eligibility verification?

AThe patient's diagnosis codes
BThe provider's NPI number
CThe CPT code for the planned procedure
DWhether the patient's insurance plan is active and what benefits, copays, and deductibles apply
✅ Correct! Eligibility verification focuses on the patient's coverage: Is the plan active? What does it cover? What are the copay, deductible, and coinsurance amounts? Are referrals or preauthorizations needed? Diagnosis codes and CPT codes are clinical/coding matters, and the NPI is a provider identifier—none are verified during eligibility.
❌ The answer is D. Eligibility verification is about the patient's insurance coverage status and benefits. Codes (ICD, CPT) are assigned during coding. The NPI is a provider number used on claims. Eligibility focuses on: Is the plan active? What does it cover? What does the patient owe?
Application

💰 Step 3: Charge Capture

Charge capture is the process of recording all billable services, procedures, and supplies provided during a patient encounter. This typically happens through encounter forms (superbills), EHR documentation, or operative reports. If a service isn't captured, it can't be billed—and the practice loses revenue.

Ensure that every service, procedure, and supply is documented on the encounter form or in the EHR. Compare the provider's documentation against what was checked off on the superbill. Missing charges = lost revenue.
Recognition

What is the primary tool used for charge capture in many outpatient practices?

AThe remittance advice
BThe encounter form (superbill) or EHR charge entry
CThe CMS-1500 claim form
DThe aging report
✅ Correct! The encounter form (superbill) lists common procedures and diagnoses for the practice. The provider checks off what was done during the visit. In EHR systems, charges may be captured electronically. The CMS-1500 is the claim form (used later in submission), and the aging report tracks unpaid claims.
❌ The answer is B. The encounter form (superbill) or EHR charge entry captures what services were performed. The CMS-1500 is used to submit the claim (Step 5). The remittance advice comes from the payer (Step 7). The aging report tracks outstanding accounts.
Application

A provider performed a wound closure during an office visit but the encounter form only shows the office visit code. What revenue cycle problem does this create?

BThe patient will be overcharged
CThe wound closure will not be billed, resulting in lost revenue for the practice — this is a charge capture failure
DThe payer will add the wound closure automatically
✅ Correct! If a service is performed but not captured on the encounter form, it will never reach the claim. The practice loses revenue for work already done. This is why charge capture review — comparing documentation to charges — is essential. The payer only pays for what's on the claim.
❌ The answer is C. Uncaptured charges = unbilled services = lost revenue. The payer won't add services automatically. The claim won't be denied (it just won't include the procedure). The patient won't be overcharged—they'll actually be underbilled. This is a charge capture failure.
In real offices, a billing specialist may compare the provider's progress notes with the encounter form to catch missed charges. This "charge reconciliation" process catches revenue leaks and ensures completeness before coding begins.

🏷️ Step 4: Coding

Coding translates the provider's clinical documentation into standardized codes. ICD-10-CM codes describe diagnoses. CPT codes describe procedures. HCPCS Level II codes describe supplies, equipment, and non-physician services. Modifiers provide additional information about how a service was performed. Accurate coding is essential for clean claims and proper reimbursement.

Code to the highest level of specificity supported by documentation. Never code from assumptions—only from what the provider documented. Query the provider if documentation is unclear or incomplete.
Recognition

Which code set is used to report patient diagnoses on a claim?

AICD-10-CM
BCPT
CHCPCS Level II
DNPI
✅ Correct! ICD-10-CM (International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision, Clinical Modification) is the code set for diagnoses. CPT codes describe procedures, HCPCS Level II covers supplies/equipment/drugs, and NPI is a provider identifier—not a code set.
❌ The answer is A — ICD-10-CM. ICD-10-CM = diagnoses (why the patient was seen). CPT = procedures (what was done). HCPCS II = supplies/equipment. NPI = provider number. Know which code set does what.
Application

✅ Correct! In outpatient coding, you do not code uncertain, probable, or rule-out diagnoses. Never leave the diagnosis blank—use the documented symptoms.
❌ The answer is C. Outpatient coding guidelines say: do NOT code uncertain/possible/probable diagnoses. Code the symptoms instead (e.g., cough, fever) or query the provider. Coding an unconfirmed diagnosis risks inaccuracy and compliance issues. Leaving it blank would cause a claim rejection.
Exam trap: "The documentation says 'rule out diabetes.' What should the coder do?" In outpatient settings, code the symptoms (e.g., polyuria, polydipsia), NOT diabetes. In inpatient settings, uncertain diagnoses CAN be coded. The CBCS exam focuses on outpatient — remember this rule.

📤 Step 5: Claim Submission

Claim submission is the process of preparing and transmitting the completed claim (CMS-1500 for professional claims, UB-04 for institutional) to the payer. Claims can be submitted electronically via a clearinghouse (most common), direct data entry to the payer portal, or by paper mail. A clearinghouseA third-party organization that receives claims from providers, scrubs them for errors, reformats them to payer specifications, and transmits them to the appropriate insurance company. scrubs the claim for errors before forwarding it to the payer.

Before submitting: verify all required CMS-1500 fields are complete, check that diagnosis and procedure codes are linked correctly, confirm the correct payer is selected, and ensure timely filing deadlines will be met.
Recognition

What is the role of a clearinghouse in the claim submission process?

ATo diagnose the patient's condition
BTo determine how much the payer should pay
CTo receive claims from providers, scrub them for errors, and transmit them to the correct payer in the proper format
DTo collect payments from patients
✅ Correct! A clearinghouse acts as an intermediary. It receives electronic claims from the provider's billing system, checks them for errors (scrubbing), converts them to the standard EDI format (837P for professional claims), and forwards them to the appropriate payer. This reduces rejection rates and speeds processing.
❌ The answer is C. Clearinghouses are intermediaries between providers and payers. They scrub claims for errors, reformat them, and route them to the correct payer. They don't diagnose patients, determine payment amounts, or collect from patients.
Application

📤 Claim Submission — How Claims Travel

Once the claim is prepared with all required data, it is transmitted to the payer. The most common route is electronic submission via a clearinghouse. The clearinghouse scrubs the claim for formatting errors, converts it to the EDI 837Electronic Data Interchange format 837 — the standard electronic format for submitting professional (837P) or institutional (837I) claims to payers. format, and routes it to the correct payer.

Electronic claims are processed faster, have fewer errors, and generate electronic acknowledgments so the office can track submission status. Paper claims take longer and have higher error rates.

⚖️ Adjudication — The Payer's Decision Process

Adjudication is the process the payer performs to evaluate a claim. The payer checks: Is the patient covered? Is the provider in-network? Is the service covered? Does the diagnosis support medical necessity? Do the codes pass NCCI edits, LCD, and NCD rules? The result is a decision to pay, deny, or partially pay.

Adjudication is NOT something the provider's office does. The payer adjudicates. The office submits, then waits. Many students confuse "processing the claim" (payer) with "submitting the claim" (provider).

✅ Correct! Adjudication is performed by the payer. They review the claim against the patient's policy, benefit rules, coding edits, and medical necessity guidelines.
❌ The answer is C — The payer. The office submits; the clearinghouse transmits; the payer adjudicates. Don't confuse the roles.

❌ The answer is B. Adjudication is the payer's evaluation of whether a claim qualifies for payment and how much. Coding is done by the office. Copay collection is a front-end task.

📩 Remittance — The Payer's Report Card

After adjudication, the payer sends a remittance advice — either an ERAElectronic Remittance Advice (EDI 835) — shows what was paid, denied, adjusted, and the patient's responsibility. or a paper EOBExplanation of Benefits — paper version of the payer's payment decision sent to provider and/or patient.. The remittance tells the office: what was paid, what was denied, why (using CARC codesClaim Adjustment Reason Codes — standardized codes explaining why a claim was adjusted or denied.), adjustments made, and what the patient owes.

The remittance advice is the single most important document after a claim is processed. It tells you everything about the payer's decision. Without reading it carefully, errors go unnoticed and revenue is lost.

💳 Step 8: Payment Posting

Payment posting is the process of recording payments, adjustments, and denials to the patient's account based on the remittance advice. This includes posting the payer's payment, applying contractual adjustmentsThe difference between the billed amount and the allowed amount per the provider's contract with the payer. This amount is written off and cannot be billed to the patient. (write-offs), recording denials, and identifying the patient's remaining balance (copay, deductible, coinsurance). Accuracy is critical — posting errors create incorrect patient balances and accounting problems.

Before posting: verify the patient name, account number, and date of birth match the remittance. Post the payer payment, write off the contractual adjustment, and transfer the patient responsibility to the patient balance. Never post without verifying the patient identity first.

✅ Correct! Before posting, always verify patient identity: name, account number, and DOB. Posting to the wrong account creates balance errors, incorrect patient statements, and accounting problems that are difficult to untangle.
❌ The answer is A. Always verify the patient's identity (name, account number, DOB) before posting. This prevents misapplied payments. Appointment dates, license numbers, and clearinghouse numbers are not relevant to payment posting verification.

📝 Step 9: Appeals

An appeal is a formal request to the payer to reconsider a claim that was denied or underpaid. Appeals must include supporting documentation: medical records, operative notes, a letter explaining why the service was medically necessary, corrected coding if applicable, and any other evidence that supports the claim. Appeals have deadlines — filing late forfeits the right to appeal.

Appeals exist because payers sometimes make errors. A denied claim is not always correctly denied. The billing specialist should always review the denial reason, determine if the denial is valid, and appeal when the office believes the claim should have been paid.

✅ Step-by-Step Reminders

1. Registration: Collect demographics, insurance cards, IDs, AOB, consent forms.
2. Eligibility: Verify active coverage, benefits, copay/deductible, COB, referral/auth needs.
3. Charge Capture: Document all services on encounter form/EHR. If not captured, it can't be billed.
4. Coding: Assign ICD-10-CM (diagnosis), CPT (procedures), HCPCS II (supplies), modifiers. Code to highest specificity from documentation.
5. Submission: Transmit CMS-1500/837P via clearinghouse. Clearinghouse scrubs for errors.
6. Adjudication: PAYER reviews claim — verifies coverage, edits, medical necessity. Payer decides to pay, deny, or adjust.
7. Remittance: Payer sends ERA (835) or EOB showing payment, denials, adjustments, patient responsibility.
8. Posting: Post payer payment, contractual adjustment (write-off), patient balance. Verify patient identity first.
9. Appeals: Challenge denials with documentation. Determine: was the payer wrong (appeal) or was the claim wrong (correct & resubmit)?
10. Collections: Pursue patient balances via statements, calls, payment plans. Last resort: collections agency (follow FDCPA).

📄 Claims & Adjudication Logic Summary

Clean claim: Submitted without errors, passes all edits, processed on first submission.
Rejection vs. Denial: Rejection = blocked before payer review (data errors). Denial = payer reviewed and said no.
Rejection fix: Correct the error, resubmit.
Denial fix: Review denial reason → correct & resubmit OR appeal with documentation.
EDI 837: Electronic claim TO payer. EDI 835: Electronic remittance FROM payer.
270/271: Eligibility inquiry/response. 276/277: Claim status inquiry/response.
CARC codes: On remittance — explain WHY the claim was adjusted or denied.
Contractual adjustment: Billed − Allowed = write-off (cannot bill patient).
Patient responsibility: Copay + deductible + coinsurance (DO collect from patient).

💰 Payment Math Formula

Billed Amount − Allowed Amount = Contractual Adjustment (write off)
Allowed Amount × Coinsurance % = Patient Coinsurance
Allowed Amount − Patient Share = Payer Payment
Payer Payment + Patient Responsibility + Write-off = Billed Amount ✓

⚠️ Top 10 Exam Traps

1. Confusing rejection (before payer) with denial (after payer review).
2. Thinking adjudication is done by the office — it's done by the PAYER.
3. Forgetting that eligibility verification includes referral/auth checks, not just active coverage.
4. Coding uncertain/probable diagnoses in outpatient settings — code symptoms instead.
5. Writing off patient copay/coinsurance — this can be fraud.
6. Appealing when you should correct & resubmit (claim error vs. payer error).
7. Sending to collections before appealing a denied claim.
8. Billing the patient before submitting to secondary insurance.
9. Resubmitting a claim without checking status first (creates duplicates).
10. Confusing the birthday rule — it's month/day, NOT birth year.

⚖️ Key Laws & Regulations Cheat Sheet

HIPAA: Protects patient health information (PHI). Privacy Rule, Security Rule, breach notification.
HITECH Act: Strengthened HIPAA — higher penalties, business associate requirements, breach notification mandates.
False Claims Act: Prohibits submitting false claims to government. Qui tam (whistleblower) provision.
Stark Law: Prohibits physician self-referrals for Medicare/Medicaid designated health services.
Anti-Kickback Statute: Prohibits offering/receiving payment for referrals of federal healthcare patients.
FDCPA: Regulates third-party debt collection — no harassment, restricted hours, written validation required.
EMTALA: Requires emergency departments to treat all patients regardless of ability to pay.
OIG: Investigates fraud, waste, abuse. Publishes Work Plan. Maintains exclusion list.
RAC: Recovery Audit Contractor — audits Medicare claims for overpayments/underpayments.
